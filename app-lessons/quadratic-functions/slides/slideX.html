2.) Let's introduce a slightly more general quadratic function:

We will just multiply $$x^2$$ by a constant number.

$$f(x) = 2x^2$$

What happened to the graph? Does it still look like a parabola? Is it "narrower" or "wider" than $$x^2$$ parabola? Still a parabola, but narrower. It is because for each value of $$x$$, the value of $$2x^2$$ is twice the value of the basic (canonical) parabola.

8.) Quadratic Equation

Finally, we introduce the a general form of a quadratic function:

$$f(x) = ax^2 + bx +c$$

So we added a term bx. You may think of f(x) as a sum of 2 functions:
$$g(x) = ax^2$$
$$h(x) = bx+c$$
$$f(x) = g(x) + h(x)$$

$$f(x) = $$ $$ ax^2 $$ $$ + $$ $$bx$$ $$ + $$ $$c$$

9.) Consider the following

$$h(x)$$ is a linear function. Its graph is a straight line. For instance $$h(x) = 3x+1$$

As we know, to obtain the graph of $$f(x)$$ we need, for each $$x$$, to add values of $$g(x)$$ and $$h(x)$$. The graph of $$g(x)$$ is a nice symmetric parabola. But the line $$h(x)$$ is angled up, so as we trend to the right in $$x$$, we add bigger and bigger values to the $$g(x)$$, and that may affect the shape of parabola. But how?

Does it still look like a parabola? Is it still symmetric? Is it wider or narrower than $$g(x) = ax^2$$? Do the branches extend up or down?
